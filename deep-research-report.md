## Flow Boundary

The attached reference document and workflow diagrams describe a single unified food-order process in which the admin/master system defines the menu once, then both the POS Terminal and the customer-facing website consume that same published structure. In that model, an orderable food item is never just a name and a price. It is built as a chain of category, base item, variation, modifier groups, add-ons, pricing rules, availability rules, and kitchen routing. That shared structure is what allows very different items such as pizza, curry, chips, naan, burgers, and rolls/wraps to move through one common order engine without price mismatch or fulfilment confusion. fileciteturn0file0

In operational terms, the food-order flow starts before the customer or cashier selects anything. The real beginning is menu definition in the admin layer, because that is where mandatory choices, optional extras, pricing logic, channel visibility, stock or sold-out behaviour, and prep-station routing are established. Only after that definition is published do the POS Terminal and website expose the item for ordering. fileciteturn0file0

## AdminPOS Creates the Orderable Item

The first live stage in the flow is menu authoring. The admin/master system creates food categories such as Pizza, Curries, Burgers, Sides, Bread & Sides, and Rolls & Wraps, then creates the base items inside those categories. Examples in the source include Margherita and Pepperoni pizza, Lamb Rogan Josh, Chips, Classic Beef Burger, Naan Bread, and Chicken Roll. At this point the item shell exists, but it still is not a complete sellable order path until its ordering logic is attached. fileciteturn0file0

The next stage is variation setup, because complex food ordering depends on a price-bearing starting choice. For pizza, that is size. For curry, it is portion. For burgers, it is patty build. For naan, it is flavour/type. For chips, it is regular or large. For rolls and wraps, it is the chosen base such as paratha, tortilla, or naan roll. These variations either hold the base price or carry the price delta that establishes the correct starting total for the line item. fileciteturn0file0

After variations, the admin layer attaches modifier groups. This is the core of customisation handling. Modifier groups can be required or optional, single-select or multi-select, and can enforce minimum selections, maximum selections, free allowances, option-level prices, channel visibility, and display order. That is how the system can make crust and sauce mandatory for pizza, spice level mandatory for curry, bun and sauce mandatory for burgers, while still allowing optional extras such as extra cheese, raita, dips, chutneys, or premium burger add-ons. The same source document also shows reuse: for example, naan exists as a standalone item but is also referenced as an accompaniment within the curry flow. fileciteturn0file0

The final part of item creation is operational control. The admin/master panel assigns pricing rules, links recipe or inventory consumption where relevant, sets time/day/channel availability, marks whether the item is visible on POS, website, or both, and assigns kitchen routing such as Pizza Station, Grill, Curry Station, or KDS. Once published, that configuration is synchronised outward so both ordering channels reflect the same selling logic. fileciteturn0file0

## Customer-Facing Website Order Capture

On the website, the food-order flow is presented as a simplified customer experience, but it still follows the exact admin logic underneath. The customer browses categories, opens an item card, and enters an item detail or customisation modal. The first required interaction is usually the priced variation selection, such as size or portion. The next stage is required modifier capture, where the customer must complete mandatory choices before continuing. The following stage is optional add-ons, where toppings, sauces, extras, accompaniments, dips, and upgrades can be selected. A final notes or special-instructions field can then be used before the item is added to cart. fileciteturn0file0

The website maintains a running total during this process. That is essential because the final sold price is not just the base item price. It is the combination of the item’s variation price plus any paid modifier choices, premium add-ons, or upgrade surcharges. The source document illustrates this clearly through examples such as pizza with deep-pan crust and extra toppings, curries with accompaniments and add-ons, and burgers with cheese and premium extras. fileciteturn0file0

The public-facing website also applies a strict visibility split between customer information and internal operational data. The customer should see the item name, image, description, price-bearing options, mandatory choices, optional extras, allergens, notes field, running total, and sold-out state. The customer should not see kitchen routing, internal item codes, inventory counters, or POS tag mappings. In other words, the website exposes the order path but hides the operational internals behind that path. fileciteturn0file0

Once the configured food item is added to cart, the cart line must carry the full customisation payload rather than only the base name. A valid pizza line therefore includes size, crust, sauce, toppings, extras, and notes. A valid curry line includes portion, spice level, accompaniment, extras, and notes. Checkout then captures fulfilment mode such as delivery or collection, address where necessary, and payment method including card wallets or cash where enabled. Once submitted, the order becomes a confirmed operational order rather than a menu interaction. fileciteturn0file0

## POS Terminal Order Capture

At the POS Terminal, the order flow is staff-guided instead of customer self-directed, but it uses the same published configuration. Staff open the POS, tap the relevant category tile, tap the item tile, and the system then forces the same sequence of decisions that was built in the admin layer: required variation first, then required modifiers, then optional add-ons, and finally any free-text instruction. This matters for walk-in, phone, and counter ordering because it reduces missed questions and prevents incomplete food lines from reaching the kitchen. fileciteturn0file0

The POS flow also standardises the order-line label. After configuration, the line item shown in the basket is not left as a generic item name. It becomes an expanded operational description, such as a large pepperoni pizza with deep-pan crust and extra cheese, or a double chicken burger with cheddar, BBQ and mayo, bacon, fried egg, and a no-gherkins note. That enriched line wording is what makes downstream payment review, receipt printing, and kitchen handoff accurate. fileciteturn0file0

After all required items are entered, the POS order moves to payment. The reference flow explicitly supports cash, card-terminal, and split-payment handling. Once the order is confirmed, the POS prints the customer receipt and also produces the kitchen ticket containing quantity, item name, chosen variation, required modifiers, optional extras, accompaniments, sauces, and notes. Because the POS does not create its own menu logic, it cannot diverge from the admin definition. The same modifier rules that were configured once are what the cashier is forced to follow during live order entry. fileciteturn0file0

## Kitchen Dispatch and Live Status Propagation

After payment or order confirmation, the flow moves into production and status orchestration. The architecture shown in the reference maps Admin Panel, MongoDB Atlas, Redis Cache, POS Terminal, KDS Screen, and Customer Website into one synchronised loop. Menu changes in the admin layer invalidate cached data and push fresh menu configuration to the ordering surfaces, while customer or POS orders publish a new order event into the live queue. fileciteturn0file0

Kitchen routing is part of the order process itself, not a separate afterthought. Because each food item is assigned to a prep destination during configuration, a single customer order containing pizza, burger, curry, naan, and chips can still be captured as one order while being routed correctly to the relevant prep stations or KDS destinations. The routing only works if the ticket includes every selected modifier and note, so the kitchen makes the exact sold version of the food rather than a default version of the menu item. fileciteturn0file0

The fulfilment side then advances the order through visible status states. The customer-facing website shows a tracking journey such as Processing, Preparing, Out for Delivery, and Delivered, while the operational side includes staff actions such as bumping tickets to Ready. The source diagram shows those state changes being published live so the front end remains aligned with the kitchen and dispatch state. fileciteturn0file0

## Rules That Keep Complex Food Orders Accurate

The same flow can handle pizza, curries, chips, naan, burgers, and rolls because each item type is normalised into the same ordering pattern. Pizza is the deepest example because it layers size, crust, sauce, toppings, and extras. Burgers also have a layered flow with patty build, bun, sauce, cheese, toppings, and premium add-ons. Curries depend on portion, spice level, accompaniments, and extra sides. Chips are lighter, mostly size plus seasoning and sauces. Naan is both a standalone sellable item and a reusable accompaniment reference. Rolls and wraps combine base type, filling, spice level, salad, sauce, and extras. The ordering engine does not need separate logic for each cuisine type because the variation-plus-modifier model handles all of them. fileciteturn0file0

The source material highlights a set of control rules that must be preserved end to end if the food-order process is to remain reliable. Required selections must block progression until they are completed. Single-select groups must stop mutually exclusive choices from being applied together. Multi-select groups can be capped to protect intended builds and pricing. Free allowances let some options remain included before surcharges begin. Channel visibility controls whether an item appears on POS, website, or both. Availability scheduling ensures some items only appear during valid hours. Sold-out handling prevents items from being ordered when the kitchen should not produce them. fileciteturn0file0

The same principle also applies to combos and meal deals. In the reference design, a combo is not treated as one flat SKU. It is a parent bundle with required child selections such as burger, side, and drink, plus upgrade options where applicable. That means the food-order flow must continue collecting required choices within the bundle, and any selected child item can still carry its own modifiers. So even inside a combo, the customisation engine remains active and the process flow remains consistent. fileciteturn0file0

The final process, therefore, is straightforward in principle even though the food items are complex in practice: define the food item and its rules once in AdminPOS, publish the same configuration to POS Terminal and website, force the orderer through the required and optional customisation path in sequence, calculate price dynamically from the exact selections, route the confirmed order to the correct kitchen destination with full modifier detail, and keep live status aligned until the order is fulfilled. fileciteturn0file0
